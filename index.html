Mutations

Although the process of DNA translation is highly efficient and precise, mistakes can often happen causing mutation. The most common type of mutation is point mutation, where a single base is added, removed, or substituted for another. Substituting nucleotide bases can cause missense mutations which is when a different amino acid is incorporated into the protein, potentially altering the proteinâ€™s function. A silent mutation happens if the substituted base corresponds to the same amino acid as the original DNA. Alternatively, a nonsense mutation can happen if the codon is changed to a stop codon, leading to premature termination of translation and an often non-functional protein. Frameshift mutations are by far the most impactful, and they happen when a base is added or removed. These mutations alter the reading frame of mRNA changing the entire downstream sequence, often resulting in a different non-functional protein.
